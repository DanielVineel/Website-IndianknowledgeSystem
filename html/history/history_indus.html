Indus Valley Civilization

The Indus Valley Civilization (circa 3300–1300 BCE) was one of the world's earliest urban civilizations, located in present-day Pakistan and northwest India. It is known for its advanced city planning, trade, and culture.

Major Cities

  • Harappa: One of the first discovered cities, known for well-planned streets and granaries.
  • Mohenjo-Daro: Famous for the Great Bath, advanced drainage systems, and organized layout.
  • Dholavira & Lothal: Key trading and administrative centers.

Society and Economy

  • People practiced agriculture, cultivating wheat, barley, and cotton.
  • Trade flourished with Mesopotamia and other regions.
  • Craftsmen produced pottery, jewelry, and seals.
  • Society appeared relatively egalitarian with no evidence of extreme wealth gaps.

Urban Planning and Technology

  • Well-planned cities with grid layouts.
  • Advanced drainage and sewage systems.
  • Standardized weights and measures for trade.
  • Use of baked bricks and organized construction methods.

Culture and Writing

  • Developed the Indus script (yet undeciphered).
  • Religious practices included worship of nature and fertility symbols.
  • Art included terracotta figurines, pottery, and seals depicting animals and symbols.

Decline

  • Possible causes: climate change, flooding, river shifts, or invasions.
  • Gradual abandonment of major cities by 1300 BCE.

Conclusion

The Indus Valley Civilization represents a remarkable early urban culture with advanced planning, trade, and social organization, laying foundations for later Indian civilizations.
